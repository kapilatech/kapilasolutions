Our Thought Leaders at Aspen Ideas Festival 2011

Tweet
Posted by boozallen.com  on July 12, 2011




Moderator Peter Trick (R) and panelists (R to L) Cory Booker, Antonio Villaraigosa, and Gavin Newsom

Booz Allen Senior Vice President Peter Trick moderated a discussion with three big city mayors titled “Could It Be: Reimagining the Infrastructure and Institutions of America’s Cities in the 21st Century” on July 2, 2011, at the Aspen Institute’s Aspen Ideas Festival. The panelists included Cory Booker, mayor of Newark, NJ; Antonio Villaraigosa, mayor of Los Angeles; and Gavin Newsom, former mayor of San Francisco and current lieutenant governor of California. Trick shares his observations on the discussion below.

 

 

You had a panel filled with big personalities discussing some very big issues. What were your takeaways from that discussion?

One that was fascinating to me was the sense of competition between the cities to try and best each other in solving their problems. Another one that I think is very important for the country to think about is the role of “city-states.” Essentially, this was a model that was used in ancient Greece and Rome and elsewhere in Europe. The question we need to ask is how much power and how much economic development can be driven at the municipal level.

Do you see the culture of competition between cities playing an important role in America’s ability to reimagine its infrastructure?

Competition stimulates the best ideas in so many ways, so I’m absolutely convinced that this notion of competition between cities, drawing on best practices from each other to try and outdo each other, is going to be a real ingredient in America’s transformation.

The mayor of Newark brought up the innovative and cross-partisan partnerships used for public parks improvements in his city. Is that a model that will also work for larger, more traditional infrastructure projects in terms of modeling and development?

One of the essential things that came out of the conversation was the importance of bipartisanship at the state and municipal levels. It is interesting to watch Cory Booker, a Democrat, working with Chris Christie, a conservative Republican governor, to do a lot of good work in Newark, albeit on a smaller scale. The hope out of the audience was that we can use the kind of bipartisanship that’s occurring in our cities to demonstrate solutions to these important issues for decision-makers at the federal level.

How do the themes discussed today support the themes in the article “Reimagining Infrastructure,” by Booz Allen Executive Vice President Mark Gerencser, which was distributed in advance of the panel you facilitated?

Our belief is that a megacommunity is going to be necessary to solve the infrastructure problem, and what we saw with the panel was the key role that cities are going to play in achieving that. In our conversation, the audience had a nice introduction to the public-private partnerships, philanthropy, and the political courage that it’s going to take to address the infrastructure issue.

In using Booz Allen’s megacommunity concept to help solve these problems, who are the partners involved, and what is Booz Allen’s role in convening them?

A good example is the work we did with federal government on next generation aviation. We worked at the federal level; we worked with airport managers; we worked with the private sector—the airlines and the equipment manufacturers. Essentially, Booz Allen facilitated a discussion where everyone came to understand the interdependence, and then helped design and implement policies and practices that bring together people who are willing to make shared investments, so we can move our country forward.
