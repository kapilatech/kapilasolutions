Values at Work: Linking Purpose, Productivity, and Performance

Tweet
Posted by BoozAllen.com  on July 03, 2014


Moderator Kevin Delaney (l) and panelists (l to r) Dave Dillon, Horacio Rozanski, Roger Martin, and Sally Blount

Booz Allen President and Chief Operating Officer Horacio Rozanski sat on a panel at the 2014 Aspen Ideas Festival titled “Values at Work: Linking Purpose, Productivity, and Performance” on July 2. Panel members examined whether firms that put an emphasis on their core values deliver greater long-term financial results than firms that focus on short-term gains in the stock price. They also discussed the role values play in successful firms, and how that translates to financial success.

The conversation was moderated by Quartz Editor-in-Chief Kevin Delaney, with additional panelists Dave Dillon, chairman of the Kroger Co.; Sally Blount, dean of the Kellogg School of Management at Northwestern University; and Roger Martin, former dean of the Rotman School of Management at the University of Toronto. 

Held in the Aspen Institute’s flagship Paepcke Auditorium, the panel drew nearly 200 people. Following the discussion, Rozanski shared some of his insights.

What was your takeaway from the panel?

This is a very complicated subject, and we must create a narrative that integrates what we do for our investors, what we do for our employees, and what we do for our clients. With balance, we can generate a sustainable enterprise that takes care of all those constituencies.

Did your view change on this topic as a result of the panel?

We started the conversation with the premise that companies have a purpose— a single purpose. I had a lot of trouble articulating that proposition to myself. I had never thought about it that way. By the time the discussion was over, I knew that we at Booz Allen have many purposes, and that they need to be in concert with our values. As I said on the panel, the internal communications at a company are its external communications.

You say at Booz Allen you have many purposes. What are they?

We want to serve the nation. We want to do powerful work for our clients. We want to create a sustainable enterprise. We want to be our clients’ essential partner. We want to bring innovation to our clients and the world. We want to create an environment where people are excited about coming to work, and are proud about being associated with the firm. Those purposes—and those are just a few I could list—are compatible and reinforce each other. If we do all of those things we generate shareholder value.

Booz Allen is known for working very closely with clients in order to help them achieve their goals. Can you talk more about that?

I want to dissect this notion of being the “essential partner.” In those two words you can pack so much meaning—it’s beautiful. Think of running a bakery with a partner: one of you isn’t going to get rich while the other is starving. Either you’re going to rise together or you’re going fall together. At Booz Allen, when we partner with our clients, their mission becomes our mission. Either we accomplish that mission together, or we shouldn’t be around.

How does Booz Allen translate its values and purpose into stakeholder value?

You need a strong value proposition for each constituency at the company— clients, employees, and investors. With clients, they are at the center of our company matrix. Our success depends on delivering high value services that meet or exceed their expectations.

With employment, we have people coming out of the military who want to continue to serve the nation, and people coming out of college who want to save the world. We’ve got to create a culture and environment that makes them feel like they are doing that.

With our investors, it is important we deliver results for them. We have significantly cut costs over the last three years to align the firm with the budget pressures our clients are facing, while still being able to deliver the results they expect. We need to please our investors, or we won’t have the freedom to do all the things we want to do as a firm.
