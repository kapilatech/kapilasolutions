    Predicting Ocean Health

    February 24, 2015

    Skill Sets

    Share

    X

    With nearly 1,000 competitors, the National Data Science Bowl is working to help scientists assess the health of the oceans.

    Splashing in the waves at the beach, it’s hard to imagine the complex ecosystem that swirls around you. Researchers at the Hatfield Marine Science Center, however, are looking closer at plankton for clues about the oceans’ health. Although you can barely see them, the tiny plankton are critically important. They use 25 percent of CO2 released from burning fossil fuels every year and form the foundation for both marine and terrestrial food chains. 

    Because plankton are susceptible to small changes in temperature or water chemistry, the populations indicate the broader ocean health with rolling impacts on the environment, economics, and even tourism.

    Hatfield researchers have been capturing underwater images to understand, at a microscopic level, what’s happening beneath the waves. But critical insights are slow to reveal themselves; a day’s worth of images can take up to a year to analyze manually, making it nearly impossible to be of real use in protecting the oceans, forecasting changes, or correcting problems.

    The challenge hidden within the waves

    Looking for Patterns

    The plankton problem caught the attention of another group of scientists—data scientists. Although they didn’t know much about the sea, they knew that their emerging practice looks to look for patterns and ask the kinds of questions that make sense to solve world problems—including in the oceans. From this, the inaugural National Data Science Bowl was created.

    The 90-day competition, sponsored by Booz Allen Hamilton and Kaggle, provides seasoned pros and amateurs alike the opportunity to use data science to create impact on a global scale. Participants are tasked with examining nearly 100,000 underwater images and developing an algorithm that will allow researchers at the Hatfield Marine Science Center to identify and monitor plankton. 

    The top three algorithm creators and the leading university team will be awarded cash prizes totaling $175,000. These teams are also given the opportunity work with master-level data scientists.

    Solving Global Problems

    Tomorrow’s Challenge

    Data is key to everything we do. At Booz Allen, we’ve built a team of data scientists centered on the “X” factor of the future—curiosity.  That curiosity, partnered with our technological progression, has created a new community of problem solvers. The emerging data science field is collaborative, driven by the first true generation of data scientists who thrive on networking and social responsibility.  

    Sponsorship of the National Data Science Bowl is part of Booz Allen’s ongoing commitment to supporting data science education and awareness, an initiative that created the Explore Data Science training program and The Field Guide to Data Science. Booz Allen was well represented among the more than 5,000 entries submitted early in the competition.

    For more information on the competition as well as Booz Allen Hamilton and Kaggle’s support of the data science community, please visit www.datasciencebowl.com.
